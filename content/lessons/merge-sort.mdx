# Merge Sort

Merge sort is the first algorithm in this track that is provably optimal for comparison sorting:
`O(n log n)` in the **worst** case, every time, no exceptions. It is also your gateway to *divide and
conquer* — the most reusable problem-solving pattern in the toolkit. CLRS introduces it on page one
of its algorithms chapter for exactly that reason.

## Split to nothing, then merge back

<Viz algo="merge-sort" frame={14} caption="The recursion splits the array down to single elements (trivially sorted), then merges sorted runs back upward." />

The idea is two lines:

- **Divide:** cut the array in half until each piece has one element (a one-element array is already
  sorted).
- **Conquer:** merge two sorted halves into one sorted whole by repeatedly taking the smaller front.

The merge is where the work happens, and it is *linear*: walk both halves with two fingers, always
copying the smaller front element.

## The algorithm

1. `sort(lo, hi)`: if the range has ≤ 1 element, return.
2. `mid = ⌊(lo + hi) / 2⌋`; `sort(lo, mid)`; `sort(mid, hi)`.
3. **Merge** `a[lo..mid)` and `a[mid..hi)`: compare the two fronts, append the smaller, until both
   are consumed.

<Callout kind="insight">
  **Stable, if you break ties left.** When the two fronts are equal, take from the **left** half
  (`a[i] ≤ a[j]`). That single `≤` keeps equal keys in their original order — the property that lets
  you sort by one key without scrambling a previous sort. Merge sort is the default *stable* sort in
  many standard libraries because of it.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="warning">
  **It is not in place.** The merge needs `O(n)` scratch space. On memory-tight problems that is the
  catch — quicksort sorts in place with only `O(log n)` stack. The classic trade is merge sort's
  worst-case guarantee versus quicksort's smaller footprint.
</Callout>

## Complexity

| Case | Time | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Best | `O(n log n)` | log n levels of halving, O(n) merge per level |
| Average | `O(n log n)` | the structure is input-independent |
| Worst | `O(n log n)` | the guarantee — no pathological input |
| Space | `O(n)` | scratch buffer for merging |

Why `log n` levels? Halving `n` down to 1 takes `log₂ n` steps; each level merges a total of `n`
elements. `n × log n` is the whole story, and it never degrades — the headline advantage over
quicksort.

## In interviews & contests

- **Reach for it** when you need a **guaranteed** O(n log n), stability, or you're sorting a **linked
  list** (merge sort needs no random access — it's the canonical list sort).
- **Interviewers probe:** "Stable?" (yes), "In place?" (no, O(n) extra), and the merge step itself —
  *Merge Two Sorted Lists* is the merge subroutine asked directly.
- **Contest mapping:** the merge pattern powers **counting inversions** and is the structure behind
  divide-and-conquer on ranges; `n log n` clears n ≈ 10⁶ within a second.

## Summary

- Divide to single elements, merge sorted halves with a two-finger linear scan.
- `O(n log n)` in **all** cases; stable when ties break left; needs `O(n)` scratch space.
- The merge subroutine is itself a common interview question and the engine of inversion counting.
