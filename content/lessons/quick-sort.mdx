# Quick Sort

Quicksort is the sort the world actually runs — the default inside countless standard libraries —
because in practice it is fast, in place, and cache-friendly. The twist: its worst case is `O(n²)`,
and the gap between "usually great" and "occasionally terrible" is entirely about how you pick the
**pivot**. Understanding that gap is the interview.

## Partition around a pivot

<Viz algo="quick-sort" frame={8} caption="The coral bar is the pivot; the scan pushes everything smaller to its left, then drops the pivot into the gap — its final position." />

One pass, called **partition**, does the real work: choose a pivot, then rearrange so everything
smaller is to its left and everything larger to its right. The pivot is now in its **final** sorted
position. Recurse on the two sides — there is no merge step.

## The algorithm (Lomuto partition)

1. `quicksort(lo, hi)`: if `lo ≥ hi`, return.
2. `pivot = a[hi]` (the last element); `i = lo − 1`.
3. For `j` from `lo` to `hi − 1`: if `a[j] < pivot`, increment `i` and swap `a[i]` with `a[j]`.
4. Swap `a[i + 1]` with `a[hi]` — the pivot lands at index `i + 1`.
5. Recurse on `a[lo .. i]` and `a[i + 2 .. hi]`.

<Callout kind="warning">
  **The sorted-input trap.** With a fixed last-element pivot, an already-sorted (or reverse-sorted)
  array makes every partition maximally lopsided — one side empty — and the recursion becomes
  `O(n²)`. This is a real interview "gotcha." Fix it with a **random** pivot or **median-of-three**,
  which make the bad case astronomically unlikely (CLRS §7.3, randomized quicksort).
</Callout>

<Callout kind="insight">
  **In place, and the basis of quickselect.** Quicksort needs only `O(log n)` stack space — no
  scratch array. Stop the recursion the moment the pivot lands on the rank you want and you get
  **quickselect**: the kth-smallest element in expected `O(n)`.
</Callout>

## Complexity

| Case | Time | When |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Best | `O(n log n)` | pivot splits the range evenly |
| Average | `O(n log n)` | random or typical pivots |
| Worst | `O(n²)` | every pivot is the min or max |
| Space | `O(log n)` | recursion stack (in place otherwise) |

Quicksort is **not stable** (the partition swaps move equal keys past each other). Its average
constant factor is smaller than merge sort's, which is why it usually wins wall-clock races despite
the scarier worst case.

## In interviews & contests

- **Reach for it** when you want an in-place O(n log n) and stability doesn't matter — or when the
  real question is **selection** (kth-largest → quickselect).
- **Interviewers probe:** the worst case and how to avoid it (random pivot), stability (no), and
  Lomuto vs **Hoare** partition (Hoare does fewer swaps).
- **Contest mapping:** you'll usually call the library sort — *introsort*: quicksort that falls back
  to heapsort to dodge O(n²). Knowing partition still matters for quickselect and Dutch-flag problems.

## Summary

- Partition places the pivot in its final spot; recurse on each side — no merge.
- Average `O(n log n)`, in place with `O(log n)` stack; **worst `O(n²)`** on bad pivots — randomize.
- Not stable; the partition routine also gives you quickselect (kth element in expected O(n)).
